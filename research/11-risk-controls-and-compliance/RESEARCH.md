# Risk Controls and Compliance Research

Status: Phase 0 research only. Phase 0 and Phase 1 must block live trading.

This document is not a legal interpretation. TRIDENT-LOB should obtain review from qualified securities counsel, tax counsel, and the selected broker before live trading is considered. The SEC says its investor publications are not legal interpretations and directs readers with legal questions to consult securities counsel: https://www.sec.gov/about/reports-publications/investor-publications/day-trading-your-dollars-at-risk

## Scope

TRIDENT-LOB is a research system for turbulent reaction-diffusion interface dynamics in electronic limit order books. The model specification says the first implementation should be used for simulation, backtesting, and paper trading, with live trading blocked by validation gates, risk limits, and broker compliance checks: ../../docs/TRIDENT_LOB_MODEL.md

No Phase 0 or Phase 1 artifact should place live trades, write live-trading code, store broker credentials, claim profitability, use future data, or recommend live deployment before explicit validation gates are satisfied. This follows the repository hard rules in ../../AGENTS.md and aligns with SEC and FINRA warnings that day trading and margin can create severe losses: https://www.sec.gov/about/reports-publications/investor-publications/day-trading-your-dollars-at-risk and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2270

## Current Regulatory Baseline

- Market access controls: any broker-dealer with market access must establish, document, and maintain risk management controls and supervisory procedures designed to manage financial, regulatory, and other risks. TRIDENT-LOB should model equivalent pre-trade controls even in paper mode: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Pre-set credit and capital thresholds: the system should reject any order intent that would exceed aggregate, sector, symbol, or other exposure thresholds because SEC Rule 15c3-5 requires controls designed to prevent orders that exceed pre-set credit or capital thresholds: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Erroneous order protection: the system should reject paper order intents that exceed price, size, duplicate-order, and short-window order-rate parameters because SEC Rule 15c3-5 specifically calls for controls to prevent erroneous and duplicative orders: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Authorized access: any future order-capable component should be disabled by default and restricted to named, approved operators because SEC Rule 15c3-5 requires access restrictions for trading systems that provide market access: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Immediate execution reports: any paper or later live order lifecycle should generate immediate post-trade reports to surveillance logs because SEC Rule 15c3-5 includes immediate post-trade execution report requirements for market access controls: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Margin baseline: before any margin simulation, TRIDENT-LOB should model Regulation T initial margin and broker house requirements. Regulation T generally sets 50 percent margin for margin equity securities, while FINRA notes brokers may impose additional requirements: https://www.federalreserve.gov/frrs/regulations/section-22012-supplement-margin-requirements.htm and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/key-topics/margin-accounts
- Day-trading rules, transition note: SEC Release 34-105226 approved FINRA SR-FINRA-2025-017 on April 14, 2026, replacing current day-trading margin provisions with intraday margin standards, but FINRA public rule and interpretation pages still contain legacy PDT text as of this research date. TRIDENT-LOB should treat broker implementation as authoritative for any future account and block live trading until the selected broker confirms the effective requirements in writing: https://www.sec.gov/rules-regulations/self-regulatory-organization-rulemaking/sr-finra-2025-017 and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/guidance/interps-4210
- Legacy PDT compatibility: until the selected broker documents otherwise, simulations should include legacy PDT constraints, including four or more day trades within five business days, more than 6 percent of trades, a $25,000 minimum equity rule, and day-trading buying power limits. This conservative compatibility check avoids underestimating broker restrictions: https://www.finra.org/investors/investing/investment-products/stocks/day-trading and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/4210
- Short sale baseline: any future short-sale simulation must model borrow availability, locate status, close-out risk, and short-sale marking. Regulation SHO requires broker-dealers to have reasonable grounds to believe a security can be borrowed and delivered before effecting a short sale, with documentation before the short sale: https://www.sec.gov/investor/pubs/regsho.htm and https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Part-242/SubjectGroup-regulation-sho-regulation-of-short-sales
- LULD and market-wide circuit breakers: simulations should account for trading pauses, unavailable exits, and price-band constraints because FINRA and SEC materials describe LULD as preventing trades in NMS stocks outside specified price bands, with market-wide halts at severe index declines: https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trf/limit-uplimit-down-luld-plan and https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/general-resources/news-alerts/alerts-bulletins/measures
- Broker API constraints: if Alpaca is used for paper trading later, order rate and buying-power assumptions should include Alpaca's 200 requests per minute trading API throttle, buying-power checks, paper trading limitations, and fill-simulation caveats: https://alpaca.markets/support/usage-limit-api-calls, https://docs.alpaca.markets/docs/trading/orders/, and https://docs.alpaca.markets/docs/trading/paper-trading/

## Risk Controls Recommended For Phase 1 Paper Mode

- Dry-run first: Phase 1 should only emit order intents to logs and paper ledgers, never to a live broker endpoint. This follows the project model scope and FINRA's warning that day trading can create large and immediate losses: ../../docs/TRIDENT_LOB_MODEL.md and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2270
- Max daily loss: paper trading should stop for the day when simulated realized plus mark-to-market loss reaches the lesser of 1 percent of paper equity or 2 times the 20-day median simulated daily loss. The regulatory basis is not the numeric threshold itself, but the need for pre-set financial exposure controls under SEC Rule 15c3-5 and the day-trading loss risks described by FINRA: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5 and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2270
- Max position size: paper mode should cap a single position at the lesser of 2 percent of paper equity notional, 5 percent of symbol average 1-minute dollar volume, or a fixed symbol cap approved in research configuration. This implements pre-set symbol and capital thresholds consistent with SEC Rule 15c3-5: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Max gross exposure: paper mode should cap total gross exposure at 25 percent of paper equity for Phase 1 until out-of-sample evidence and transaction-cost modeling justify a lower or higher research limit. Conservative leverage is recommended because SEC and FINRA warn that margin and day trading can create losses beyond initial capital: https://www.sec.gov/about/reports-publications/investor-publications/day-trading-your-dollars-at-risk and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2270
- Max exposure per symbol: paper mode should cap each symbol at 5 percent of total paper gross exposure and reject correlated same-sector concentration unless explicitly approved in the research config. This is a symbol-level version of the pre-set capital-threshold control required for market-access systems: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Max orders per minute: paper mode should cap generated order intents at 30 per minute globally and 5 per minute per symbol, below Alpaca's documented 200 requests per minute API throttle and consistent with erroneous-order and duplicative-order controls under SEC Rule 15c3-5: https://alpaca.markets/support/usage-limit-api-calls and https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Price collars: paper order intents should be rejected when the proposed limit price is outside a configurable collar around NBBO or the latest valid quote. This follows SEC Rule 15c3-5 erroneous-order controls and LULD price-band risk principles: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5 and https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trf/limit-uplimit-down-luld-plan
- Kill switches: paper mode should halt order-intent generation after daily loss breach, data staleness, model exception, impossible timestamp, future-data detection, duplicate-order burst, broker API error burst, LULD or halt state, missing borrow flag for short simulation, or manual operator halt. These conditions map to financial risk, regulatory risk, and system-failure risks described by SEC Rule 15c3-5 and FINRA Rule 2270: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5 and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2270
- Logging: every data snapshot, feature timestamp, model version, signal, risk decision, rejected intent, simulated order, simulated fill, cancellation, kill-switch activation, override, and approval should be logged immutably for replay. This supports documented supervisory controls and post-trade reporting principles in SEC Rule 15c3-5: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Manual approvals: any transition from backtest to paper trading, any change to risk caps, any enabling of short simulation, any use of margin simulation, and any later live-trading readiness review should require named human approval and a signed checklist. FINRA Rule 2130 requires approval procedures for accounts promoted for day-trading strategies, and SEC Rule 15c3-5 requires restricted access to trading systems: https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2130 and https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5

## Model And Data Failure Handling

- Model failure: if inference returns NaN, infinite values, stale model metadata, a missing calibration artifact, a feature schema mismatch, a confidence outside expected bounds, or a disagreement between model timestamp and market timestamp, the system should emit no order intent and activate a model-failure kill switch. This supports erroneous-order prevention under SEC Rule 15c3-5: https://ecfr.io/Title-17/Section-240.15c3-5
- Data-feed failure: if quotes, trades, bars, news, corporate actions, exchange status, or halt data are stale, missing, out of order, duplicated beyond tolerance, or timestamped in the future relative to decision time, the system should emit no order intent. This is necessary to avoid future data and to reduce system-failure risk described by FINRA's day-trading disclosure: ../../AGENTS.md and https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2270
- Backtest leakage control: every feature must be generated only from data available at or before the decision timestamp, including publication timestamps for news and SEC filings. This follows the repository no-future-data rule and supports the model specification's timestamped event-stream design: ../../AGENTS.md and ../../docs/TRIDENT_LOB_MODEL.md
- Halt and LULD handling: when a symbol is halted, paused, in a limit state, or missing reliable NBBO, the only permitted paper action should be no new exposure and risk-reducing simulated exits only when the market state allows them. LULD and market-wide circuit breaker sources warn that price bands and halts can prevent normal execution: https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trf/limit-uplimit-down-luld-plan and https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/general-resources/news-alerts/alerts-bulletins/measures
